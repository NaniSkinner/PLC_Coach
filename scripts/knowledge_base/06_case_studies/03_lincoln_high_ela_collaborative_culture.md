# Lincoln High School ELA: From Isolation to Collaboration

## Context

**Lincoln High School** - Grades 9-12, 1,200 students, suburban
**ELA Department** - 9 teachers, varying experience (2-25 years)

**Starting Point (2022):**
- Teachers worked in complete isolation
- No common assessments
- No shared curriculum or pacing
- Department meetings focused on textbook selection and field trips
- Student outcomes inconsistent across teachers
- New teachers struggled without support

---

## The Problem

### Teacher Autonomy as Barrier

**Department culture:** "Good teachers have autonomy to teach however they want"

**Results:**
- Students in Room 201 (Mr. Adams) had vastly different experiences than students in Room 205 (Ms. Rodriguez)
- No guaranteed curriculum - depended entirely on which teacher you got
- Juniors taking AP English shocked by expectations because their 9th-10th grade experiences varied wildly
- Department chair frustrated: "We call ourselves a department but we're just 9 people teaching in the same building"

### Catalyst for Change

**Spring 2022:** District mandated common end-of-course writing assessments for grades 9-11

**Teacher reaction:** "They want us all to give the same assessment? But we don't teach the same content!"

**Principal's response:** "That's the problem. We need guaranteed curriculum for all students."

---

## Year 1: Building Trust & Collaboration (2022-2023)

### Fall 2022: Difficult Beginnings

**First Department PLC Meeting (August):**
Principal announced: "You'll meet weekly to develop common assessments and curriculum."

**Teacher pushback:**
- "This eliminates teacher autonomy"
- "My students are different - one-size-fits-all won't work"
- "We've never done this before"

**Department Chair (Ms. Chen) to Principal:** "If you mandate this from the top down, you'll get compliance but not commitment. Let me work with the team."

### Building Foundation

**Ms. Chen's Approach:**

**Step 1: Develop Norms (September)**
- Full meeting dedicated to: "How do we want to work together?"
- Norms included: "Assume positive intent," "Challenge ideas, not people," "Start and end on time"

**Step 2: Start Small (October-November)**
- Didn't try to revamp entire curriculum
- Focused on ONE common assessment: 9th grade argument essay
- All teachers contribute items/prompts
- Collaborate on scoring rubric

**Step 3: Calibration Activity (December)**
- Team scored 6 student essays together
- Realized they defined "proficiency" very differently
- Worked through disagreements respectfully
- Developed anchor papers

**Breakthrough Moment:**
Mr. Adams (20-year veteran, initially resistant): "I've been teaching argument writing for two decades. But looking at these anchor papers, I realize I've been focused on grammar while others focus on reasoning. No wonder students are confused when they move from my class to the next level."

### Winter 2023: First Common Assessment

**January:** All 9th grade students wrote argument essay (same prompt, same rubric)

**Data Analysis Meeting:**
- Results ranged from 52% to 78% proficient across teachers
- Instead of defensiveness, genuine curiosity: "What's working in Room 205 that we could learn from?"

**Ms. Rodriguez shared her strategy:**
- Explicitly teaching claim-evidence-reasoning structure
- Using mentor texts to show strong arguments
- Multiple low-stakes drafts with feedback

**Result:** Other teachers tried her approach. Next CFA (March), proficiency rose across all classes.

### Spring 2023: Expanding Collaboration

**What started with one assessment expanded:**
- 9th grade: Common assessments on argument, informational, and narrative writing
- 10th grade: Started developing common assessments
- Teachers sharing lesson plans, rubrics, strategies
- Weekly meetings focused on student learning, not logistics

**Year 1 Outcomes:**
- 9th grade writing proficiency: 58% â†’ 71% (13-point gain)
- Teacher relationships stronger
- New teachers (hired mid-year) integrated smoothly with existing structures
- Culture shifting from "my students" to "our students"

---

## Year 2: Deepening & Expanding (2023-2024)

### Fall 2023: Systematic Approach

**Grade 9-11 common assessments established:**
- Each grade: 3 writing CFAs + 2 reading comprehension CFAs per semester
- Scoring calibrated through anchor papers
- Data analysis protocols used for all CFAs

**Extension:**
- 11th grade team developed challenge option for proficient writers
- Instead of "more writing," students analyzed published arguments for rhetorical choices

### Cross-Grade Collaboration

**New Practice:** "Vertical team meetings" twice per semester
- 9th grade teachers met with 10th grade teachers: "What do students need to be ready for you?"
- 10th met with 11th, etc.
- Result: Better alignment, less repetition, smoother transitions

### Mid-Year Results

**Writing Proficiency (December 2023):**
- Grade 9: 76% proficient
- Grade 10: 72% proficient
- Grade 11: 79% proficient

**Even more significant:** Consistency across teachers within grade level
- Grade 9: All teachers now 72-80% proficiency (vs. 52-78% two years ago)
- This meant guaranteed curriculum was actually happening

---

## Key Success Factors

### 1. Respecting Teacher Expertise

**Ms. Chen's Leadership:**
- Didn't frame collaboration as "fixing bad teachers"
- Positioned as "leveraging collective expertise"
- Celebrated what each teacher brought

### 2. Starting Small

**Not:** "Revamp everything at once"
**Instead:** "One assessment, one grade level, build from there"

**Result:** Success built confidence for expanding

### 3. Using Protocols

**Data meetings used structured protocols:**
- Prevented meetings from becoming defensive
- Ensured focus on student learning
- Made collaboration feel safe

### 4. Building on Strengths

**When Ms. Rodriguez's students performed well:**
Team response: "What can we learn from you?"
**Not:** "Why are others' students struggling?"

**Frame:** Collaboration as learning opportunity, not evaluation

### 5. Principal Support

**Principal's role:**
- Protected 60-minute weekly collaboration time
- Attended meetings occasionally (as learner, not evaluator)
- Publicly celebrated department's progress
- Didn't micromanage how team collaborated

---

## Challenges & How They Were Addressed

### Challenge 1: Veteran Teacher Resistance

**Mr. Adams (25 years experience):** "I've always taught my way. Why change now?"

**Response:** Not forced compliance. Instead:
- Invited him to share his expertise (he had great strategies)
- Showed data: inconsistency across teachers hurt students
- Gave time to see collaboration benefits before fully buying in
- Eventually became advocate: "I wish we'd done this 20 years ago"

### Challenge 2: Time Constraints

**Teachers:** "We barely have time to plan our own classes. Now we're planning together?"

**Reality check:** Collaboration initially took MORE time.

**Long-term:** Sharing resources, common assessments, collaborative problem-solving actually saved time.

**Year 2:** Teachers spending same or LESS time planning because of shared resources.

### Challenge 3: Balancing Common & Individual

**Concern:** "Will collaboration eliminate creativity?"

**Solution:**
- Common essential assessments and standards
- HOW teachers taught remained flexible
- Teachers still brought individual strengths/style
- Collaboration enhanced, didn't eliminate, autonomy

---

## Current Status (2024-2025)

**Year 3 Developments:**
- All grades 9-11 have common assessments in writing and reading
- 12th grade starting common assessments this year
- Department known in district as model for collaboration
- Teacher retention high (used to have turnover, now people want to work there)
- Student AP English scores improved (better preparation in 9-11)

**Ms. Chen:** "Two years ago we were 9 people working alone. Now we're a real department - a team. Our students benefit because they get guaranteed high-quality instruction no matter whose class they're in."

**Mr. Adams:** "I was the biggest skeptic. Now I can't imagine going back to working in isolation. Collaboration makes me a better teacher."

---

## Lessons Learned

**What worked:**
- Building trust through norms and respect
- Starting small and building progressively
- Using protocols to make collaboration productive
- Celebrating teacher expertise, not exposing weaknesses
- Patient leadership allowing culture to develop

**What didn't work initially:**
- Top-down mandate (principal learned to step back)
- Trying to do too much at once (initial overwhelm)
- Meetings without structure (early meetings were unfocused)

**Advice for other high schools:**
"High school teachers are used to autonomy. You can't force collaboration - you have to build trust first. Start with something small where teachers can see clear benefits. Use protocols so collaboration feels productive, not like more meetings. Be patient - culture change takes time."
